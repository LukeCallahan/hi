  PRINTING 1990 by  ELDENE WHITING C.G.

Dispelling the Graphologist's Enigma

  Printing is both the oldest and the newest of communication types. Since Wilhelm Preyer,(1895) a professor of physiology at Jena, Switzerland, formulated and proved the concept of writing as "brain writing", various writing styles have intrigued graphologists. Knowing that the brain develops an image, that nerve impulses impart that image to the writing field, it is easier to understand that even "mouth writing" and "foot writing" are legitimate methods of expressive movement. If all writing originates in the brain no matter what writing instrument or surface is utilized, surely printing is brain writing too. Why do graphologist's veer away from analyzing printing? What makes it different? How does the sincere analyst deal with printing?
 
  One graphologist who has often invited guest speakers to his group asks them what they do when confronted with a printing? The answer usually is: "I ask for another sample, cursively written." Even now when a student,asks how to analyze printing, too many teachers say: "Do the best you can." This quibbling is no longer necessary.
 
     In   1983 the San Diego chapter of the American Handwriting Analysis Foundation worked diligently on a research project  comparing      cursive writing with printing. (McMenamin,      Vadus,
  Whiting,      1983)   They were shocked and disappointed to find   so
  little      literature on printing. Textbooks written before      1940
  seldom mention the word. Those written after that            time touch on
  the   subject but      not in depth. Since 1970 articles and   books
  allude to printing, but none give practical            instruction on how to
  analyze it.
When the ball point pen appeared on the market, some graphologists claimed the science was doomed because they couldn't analyze pressure without the broad strokes of the nib pen. A few even gave up the practice of handwriting analysis because of this. Eventually most analysts learned to cope with the new writing tool. I believe we can cope with printing as well.  
     Perhaps an understanding of what has gone before will throw   some
     light on the subject.

Writing Through The Ages


Although printing has come into common use in America only in the past fifty years, the basic style was actually one of the first communication modes. Pictograms on cave walls began as strokes scratched on the earthen surface. Hieroglyphics were the sacred signs of the Egyptians. But about 3500 B.C. a style of writing was developed in the orient called "cuniform," meaning roughly, "wedge shape." As a practical method of writing, it not only was popular in the country of its origin, but was adopted by Sumerians, Babylonians, Assyrians, Hittites, Persians and by others in areas of the far east. At that time the "pen" was a reed or wooden stick, fashioned to imprint wedge shaped grooves on clay or wax. Skilled writers handled the rather awkward "pen and paper" to create lines of different widths. Some writing was pictorial in content, other writings were symbolic vertical lines (masculine) and triangular wedges (feminine). From this humble beginning our alphabet evolved. Some modern scholars call cuniform writing the "Roman letters" of the ancient orient.
